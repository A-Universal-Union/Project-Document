# A Universal Union: Research and Development Strategy

## 1. Purpose, Scope, and Review Posture

A Universal Union has already been described at the level of mission, ethical direction, and functional feature set. Those documents define why the project exists, what civic commitments it should preserve, and what major capabilities the system should eventually support. This document addresses a different question: how should the existing design be researched, specified, implemented, tested, reviewed, and adopted?

The purpose of this document is not to finalize the technical architecture of A Universal Union. It is to organize the path from civic design to technical specification and from technical specification to working software. It identifies the system layers that require research, the kinds of specifications those layers must eventually produce, the work packages that can be developed in parallel, the pilots that can test early assumptions, and the threat areas that should shape development from the beginning.

This document is written from a systems-engineering perspective. Its primary contribution is architectural decomposition: identifying system layers, dependencies, interfaces, development order, research requirements, and pilot pathways. It is intended to make AUU easier to criticize, validate, replace, and improve. Where it enters specialized domains such as cryptography, privacy engineering, distributed systems, secure identity, governance design, law, accessibility, or hostile-use analysis, it should invite review rather than claim final authority.

Many areas discussed here deserve expert review before they can be treated as secure, complete, or technically settled. Until that review is available, they should be treated as research targets, candidate approaches, or provisional implementation baselines. Production deployment of security-critical components would require stronger validation through qualified expert review, adversarial testing, audits, usability studies, and evidence from real-world pilots.

This review posture is not separate from the development method. It is part of the method. A project of this scope should not pretend that a civilization-scale civic system can be engineered safely from top-down design alone. Nor should it pretend that research without implementation will produce useful infrastructure. AUU requires both: a coherent design architecture and a disciplined pathway for turning that design into reviewed, testable, replaceable technical specifications.

The development strategy therefore begins from an existing civic design, subjects that design to research pressure, turns research into specifications, turns specifications into modular deliverables, tests deliverables through limited pilots, and only then moves toward broader adoption.

## 2. Existing Design Architecture

A Universal Union begins as civic architecture before it becomes technical architecture.

The existing design defines a system in which individuals can participate through protected identities and pseudonyms; communities can deliberate, poll, vote, challenge, delegate, federate, and preserve records; and collective power can become more visible without forcing ordinary people into total exposure. The central design boundary is that the system should protect persons from exposure, but not protect collective power from accountability.

This first architecture is not a protocol specification. It does not settle which cryptographic primitives should be used, which consensus system should anchor records, which zero-knowledge proof system should verify eligibility, or which networking stack should protect traffic from surveillance. It defines the civic relationships the technical system must eventually support.

At the highest level, AUU depends on several interacting layers.

The **identity layer** gives participants control over base identity, pseudonyms, credentials, recovery, and participation contexts. Its purpose is not to create a public profile, but to allow legitimate participation without unnecessary exposure.

The **trust, reputation, and standing layer** helps individuals and communities interpret participation. Trust is concerned with human recognition and Sybil resistance. Reputation concerns itself with recorded conduct, warnings, contributions, fulfilled obligations, challenges, and outcomes. Standing is the relationship between a participant and the process being affected.

The **deliberation layer** turns expression into structured civic reasoning. It supports threads, classifications, argument mapping, public challenge, synthesis, semantic discovery, and discussion-to-vote linking.

The **polling and voting layer** allows communities to measure sentiment or make formal decisions under defined rules. Polls help a community understand itself. Votes create or record authority through a recognized procedure.

The **collective and organizational layer** allows groups to define membership, roles, governance rules, roster visibility, public claims, federation relationships, and decision processes.

The **representation and delegation layer** allows participants to delegate attention or voting power while preserving reviewability and revocation.

The **records and auditability layer** preserves civic memory: decisions, votes, receipts, delegation context, public challenges, unresolved objections, governance records, and historical proofs.

The **analytics layer** helps communities interpret large-scale activity without replacing the underlying record. It may support trust-weighted interpretation, standing-aware views, ideological heat maps, semantic clustering, bridge detection, and trend analysis, but its outputs should remain contestable.

The **security and privacy layer** cuts across every other layer. It must protect identity materials, credentials, ballots, devices, recovery processes, records, software supply chains, and the disclosure boundaries between private persons and accountable civic processes.

The **network traffic privacy layer** protects persons from state surveillance and other network-level observation by reducing the ability to correlate participation through IP addresses, timing patterns, endpoint logs, DNS requests, push notifications, traffic volume, hostile local networks, repeated node connections, or monitoring by network operators.

The **network and verifiability layer** provides the infrastructure through which actions propagate, nodes coordinate, records are anchored, and clients verify that important events have not been silently altered or erased.

These layers should not be treated as isolated features. They constrain one another. Identity without privacy becomes surveillance. Privacy without accountability becomes cover for hidden power. Discussion without structure becomes noise. Voting without context becomes shallow authorization. Delegation without review becomes surrender. Analytics without contestability becomes invisible authority. Records without repair become permanent judgment. The purpose of the development strategy is to preserve this architecture while turning it into software carefully enough that the technical implementation does not betray the civic design.

## 3. Minimal System Mechanics

At minimum, AUU can be understood as a system where participants act through controlled civic identities, communities define civic processes, and public claims become reviewable without exposing more personhood than necessary. Its basic mechanics connect identity, pseudonymity, trust, standing, deliberation, polling, voting, collective governance, delegation, records, analytics, and security into one civic architecture.

A participant begins with an **identity wallet**. The wallet manages identity-related assets such as pseudonyms, credentials, trust relationships, recovery options, and participation contexts. It should not function primarily as a public profile. It should function as a civic control point that lets a participant prove what must be proven, withhold what does not, and act through different identities in different contexts.

A participant may use different **pseudonyms** for different forms of participation. At the wallet level, pseudonyms may have different privacy and visibility relationships: some may be public, private, or secret, with each intended for context-specific purposes. Other parts of the system may then place additional presentation or accountability rules around them. A discussion may display participants through randomized or neutral identities to reduce forms of bias, while delegation may require a more accountable pseudonym. For development purposes, these distinctions should remain separate: wallet-level pseudonym controls should not be confused with discussion-layer presentation choices or process-specific accountability requirements.

A participant may enter a **process** by joining a discussion, classifying a claim, opening a proposal, answering a poll, casting a vote, delegating authority, challenging a collective action, joining a collective, or contributing to a record. Some processes may require only ordinary pseudonymous participation. Others may require proof of personhood, membership, locality, credential status, affected standing, or some other eligibility condition. The system should verify only what must be verified for the process at hand.

A **collective** uses the same basic mechanisms at a group level. It may define membership rules, govern itself, open discussions, run polls, hold votes, make public claims, join federations, assign roles, issue announcements, preserve records, and receive public challenge. Where a collective participates in a larger collective, federation, jurisdictional process, or proof-gated decision, its external authority should remain scoped to the proofs required by that process. Members may participate internally under the collective’s own rules, but the system should not allow a collective to convert ineligible internal participation into eligible external voting power, representation, or standing.

A **poll** helps a community understand itself, while a **vote** creates or records authority through a recognized procedure. Both should remain connected to deliberation. Participants and observers should be able to see the proposal, eligibility rules, voting method, deadline, linked discussion, relevant arguments, public challenges, unresolved objections, results, and records. Where ballots are secret, the process should still be auditable. Where rosters are protected, participation should still be verifiable at the appropriate level.

**Trust**, reputation, and standing help interpret participation without collapsing civic judgment into one universal score. A participant may be a real person but have little standing in a specific process. A pseudonym may have strong reputation in one domain and none in another. A collective may have protected membership but still need to show why its claim of representation should be taken seriously. These distinctions should be legible rather than hidden inside platform assumptions.

The system should support multiple forms of **proof**: proof of personhood, proof of eligibility, proof of membership, proof of standing, proof of delegation, proof of inclusion, proof of process, and proof of record integrity. These proofs do not all require the same strength, privacy model, or technical implementation. Their requirements depend on context, consequence, and risk.

The development strategy must preserve **flexibility** across these mechanics. AUU should not hard-code one social theory of legitimacy into the platform. It should provide mechanisms by which communities can define, verify, contest, audit, and revise legitimacy claims according to context.

## 4. Integration Problems, Requirements, and Research Boundaries

The existing design identifies the civic mechanisms AUU must support, but those mechanisms still need implementation-facing abstractions before they can become technical specifications. The project must define how processes request proofs, accept participation, assign authority, preserve privacy, produce records, handle failure, and remain reviewable under adversarial conditions.

This section identifies the integration problems that should shape research and development. The table summarizes the major areas. The subsections that follow describe the required abstraction, design constraint, research direction, and development consequence for each area.

| Area                                           | Required abstraction                | Design constraint                                                 | Research direction                                                                 |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Eligibility without exposure                   | Eligibility-check interface         | No identity bridge                                                | Privacy-preserving credentials, proofs, revocation, receipts                       |
| Authority across contexts                      | Authority-scope model               | No authority laundering                                           | Delegation, federation, capability systems, policy languages                       |
| Privacy-preserving accountability              | Privacy-aware record model          | Reviewability without exposure                                    | Audit logs, receipts, voting records, selective disclosure                         |
| Governance configurability                     | Governance configuration schema     | No procedural illegibility                                        | Workflow engines, policy schemas, DAO tooling, civic process models                |
| Security controls, defaults, and user warnings | Security-control policy model       | Avoid unsafe defaults, excessive friction, and hidden enforcement | Biometrics, passkeys, hardware keys, cryptographic authentication, usable security |
| Failure and recovery                           | Compromise and recovery state model | No accountability escape hatch                                    | Key recovery, revocation, incident response, dispute resolution                    |
| Abuse resistance                               | Adversarial test model              | No defensive capture                                              | Threat modeling, Sybil resistance, moderation, capture resistance                  |

### 4.1 Eligibility Without Exposure

**Purpose:** Verify process eligibility without exposing the private basis of qualification.

**Required abstraction:** AUU needs an eligibility-check interface defining the criterion, verifier, proof artifact, process scope, expiry, revocation, audit metadata, participant receipt, challenge path, and mistaken-acceptance handling. New eligibility criteria should be able to implement this interface without forcing the system to store raw private information.

**Design constraint:** Because eligibility may originate in opaque real-world institutions, the interface must prevent private qualification sources from becoming identity bridges into later civic participation.

**Research directions:** Research should compare verifiable credentials, selective disclosure, anonymous credentials, zero-knowledge proofs, signed attestations, commitments, nullifiers, revocation mechanisms, and proof receipts against privacy, auditability, usability, coercion risk, implementation complexity, and adversarial behavior.

**Development consequence:** Prototype eligibility-check flows before applying them to high-stakes voting, federation, delegation, or collective authority.

### 4.2 Authority Across Contexts

**Purpose:** Define where authority applies, where it does not, and how authority changes across processes.

**Required abstraction:** AUU needs an authority-scope model defining authority source, scope, permitted actions, affected process, voting weight or standing, delegation status, aggregation rules, expiry, revocation, and rejection conditions. The system should be able to determine whether a participant, pseudonym, delegate, collective, credential, or prior decision has authority to perform a specific action in a specific process.

**Design constraint:** Because authority may be valid in one context and invalid in another, the model must prevent authority laundering across delegation chains, collective aggregation, federation membership, credential reuse, or ambiguous process rules.

**Research directions:** Research should examine authority models in federated systems, delegated voting, capability-based authorization, access-control systems, verifiable credentials, organizational governance software, and formal policy languages. The central question is how AUU can represent scoped authority clearly enough for software to enforce it, users to understand it, collectives to configure it, and outsiders to challenge it when authority is misapplied.

**Development consequence:** Specify authority scoping before federation, delegation chains, collective voting power, or representative claims become durable features.

### 4.3 Privacy-Preserving Accountability

**Purpose:** Allow private participation to produce reviewable public authority.

**Required abstraction:** AUU needs a privacy-aware record model defining what is public, what remains private, what can be proven without disclosure, what may be reviewed under restricted conditions, what produces participant receipts, and what becomes part of the durable audit trail.

**Design constraint:** Because secrecy can protect participants while weakening reviewability, the record model must preserve enough information to review authority without turning protected participation into identity, credential, association, ballot, or standing disclosure.

**Research directions:** Research should compare audit logs, transparency logs, cryptographic receipts, verifiable voting records, selective disclosure, private membership systems, and privacy-preserving dispute processes. The central question is how AUU can make authority reviewable without making protected participation publicly legible.

**Development consequence:** Test voting records, participation receipts, protected membership, credential proofs, public claims, and challenge records together rather than prototyping privacy and accountability as separate systems.

### 4.4 Governance Configurability Without Incoherence

**Purpose:** Allow communities to define their own governance processes without those processes becoming illegible.

**Required abstraction:** AUU needs a governance configuration schema defining eligibility, roles, permissions, voting method, quorum, thresholds, timelines, delegation rules, challenge paths, privacy settings, record outputs, and federation effects. The schema should be expressive enough for different communities while still producing interpretable and auditable records.

**Design constraint:** Because excessive procedural freedom can make processes difficult to understand or compare, the governance model must prevent configurability from producing technically valid but socially incomprehensible procedures.

**Research directions:** Research should examine governance configuration systems, process modeling, policy languages, workflow engines, formal rule definitions, DAO governance tooling, civic participation platforms, and human-readable audit formats. The central question is how AUU can support procedural diversity while preserving interpretability, interoperability, and reviewability.

**Development consequence:** Develop governance configuration as a constrained vocabulary rather than a loose collection of settings. Early prototypes should test whether ordinary users can understand what a configured process means before relying on it for consequential decisions.

### 4.5 Security Controls, Defaults, and User Warnings

**Purpose:** Give users and collectives meaningful control over security-sensitive choices without relying on unsafe defaults, excessive friction, or hidden platform enforcement.

**Required abstraction:** AUU needs a policy model for account security, pseudonym protection, wallet access, session authorization, credential disclosure, network traffic privacy, collective administration, warnings, confirmations, overrides, and hard restrictions. This model should define which settings are user-controlled, which are recommended, which require fresh confirmation, which require escalation, and which actions the system should refuse because the risk is too severe.

**Design constraint:** Because both weak protection and excessive friction can harm users, the policy model must avoid miscalibrated control. The system should reduce false confidence, warning fatigue, security theater, and adoption-killing complexity.

**Research directions:** Research should examine biometric authentication, passkeys/WebAuthn, hardware security keys, trusted execution environments, cryptographic authentication, device authorization, session management, key rotation, account recovery, and secure credential storage. These should be compared with human-computer interaction and usable-security research on authentication usability, security-warning effectiveness, passwordless login, account recovery failures, and user comprehension of privacy and security controls. The central question is how AUU can protect wallets, pseudonyms, sessions, and authority-bearing actions without forcing every user into security practices they do not understand or cannot sustain.

**Development consequence:** Test security, privacy, and governance controls as user-experience problems before treating them as settled policy. Early prototypes should measure comprehension, retention, error recovery, and warning fatigue alongside technical correctness.

### 4.6 Compromise, Revocation, and Continuity

**Purpose:** Handle lost, compromised, revoked, or replaced accounts, pseudonyms, credentials, devices, delegations, and collective roles without erasing accountability or preserving authority that should no longer apply.

**Required abstraction:** AUU needs a recovery and revocation state model. The system should define how wallets, pseudonyms, credentials, sessions, delegations, and collective roles move between states such as active, suspended, compromised, revoked, recovered, succeeded, or disputed. It should also define what each state does to future authority, existing records, participation receipts, reputation signals, and public warnings.

**Design constraint:** Recovery should affect future control without silently rewriting the past. A participant may need a way to regain access or mark compromise, but recovery should not erase prior records, launder reputation, transfer authority improperly, or make disputed history disappear.

**Research directions:** Research should examine key recovery, social recovery, credential revocation, account compromise workflows, session invalidation, transparency logs, incident response, fraud-resistant recovery, and dispute resolution. The central question is how AUU can restore legitimate control while preserving enough visible continuity for others to understand what changed.

**Development consequence:** Prototype recovery and revocation flows before authority-bearing features become durable. The system should be tested against ordinary loss, hostile takeover, false compromise claims, revoked credentials, delegation abuse, and disputed successor identities.

### 4.7 Adversarial Use and Abuse Resistance

**Purpose:** Test AUU against hostile use, not merely ordinary disagreement.

**Required abstraction:** AUU needs an adversarial test model defining likely attackers, attack surfaces, abuse patterns, detection limits, mitigation options, and the harms created by both action and inaction. Features that create authority, reach, visibility, durable records, or external legitimacy should be tested against hostile behavior before production use.

**Design constraint:** Because abuse controls can themselves become tools of exclusion, censorship, surveillance, factional advantage, or institutional control, the abuse-resistance model must avoid defensive capture.

**Research directions:** Research should examine threat modeling, abuse-resistant social systems, Sybil resistance, coercion resistance, spam and overload controls, adversarial governance, moderation systems, challenge mechanisms, network privacy, and capture-resistant institutional design. The central question is how AUU can resist manipulation without concentrating control in a central authority or creating tools that hostile actors can repurpose.

**Development consequence:** Let abuse resistance shape development order. Features that create authority, visibility, reach, durable records, or external legitimacy should be tested under adversarial conditions before being treated as ready for pilots.

### 5.1 Eligibility-Check Interface

**Research question:** How can AUU verify that a participant satisfies an eligibility requirement without exposing the private basis of that eligibility or creating an identity bridge between real-world qualification and civic participation?

**Starter sources:**

* [W3C Verifiable Credentials Data Model 2.0](https://www.w3.org/TR/vc-data-model-2.0/) — standard model for issuer-holder-verifier credentials.
* [W3C Verifiable Credentials Overview](https://www.w3.org/TR/vc-overview/) — readable orientation to the Verifiable Credentials family.
* [IETF RFC 9901: Selective Disclosure for JSON Web Tokens](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/rfc9901/) — standard for selectively disclosing JWT claims.
* [W3C Data Integrity BBS Cryptosuites v1.0](https://www.w3.org/TR/vc-di-bbs/) — selective disclosure and unlinkable proof work for verifiable credentials.
* [On Cryptographic Mechanisms for the Selective Disclosure of Verifiable Credentials](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.08196) — comparison of selective-disclosure mechanisms and their tradeoffs.
* [Selective disclosure of claims from multiple digital credentials](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.15447v1) — useful for thinking about multi-credential eligibility without overexposure.

**What to learn from them:** Contributors should identify which approaches support selective disclosure, unlinkable presentations, predicate proofs, expiry, revocation, duplicate-use prevention, audit metadata, and participant receipts. The key issue is not whether a credential can be verified, but whether verification can occur without allowing issuers, verifiers, platform operators, or public records to reconnect eligibility with later civic participation.

**Open evaluation questions:** Can eligibility be proven without revealing the credential source? Can duplicate participation be prevented without linking all future participation? Can revocation work without live issuer callbacks? Can public records prove that eligibility rules were applied without reconstructing private eligibility facts? Can the same proof system support low-stakes community use and high-risk protected participation?

### 5.2 Authority-Scope Model

**Research question:** How can AUU represent who is allowed to act, for whom, in which process, under which conditions, without allowing authority to leak across contexts?

**Starter sources:**

* [Open Policy Agent: Policy Language](https://openpolicyagent.org/docs/policy-language) — readable entry point for declarative policy logic using Rego.
* [OASIS XACML Technical Committee](https://www.oasis-open.org/committees/xacml/faq.php) — standard access-control policy language background.
* [RFC 7061: XACML profile for authorization requests and policies](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc7061) — useful for request-policy-response authorization models.
* [Capability Myths Demolished](https://papers.agoric.com/assets/pdf/papers/capability-myths-demolished.pdf) — classic paper clarifying capability-based security.
* [W3C Decentralized Identifiers Core](https://www.w3.org/TR/did-core/) — identity-control model relevant to authority and key control.
* [Snapshot Strategies documentation](https://docs.snapshot.box/) — practical example of configurable voting power and governance strategies.

**What to learn from them:** Contributors should compare policy-language approaches, capability-based authority, credential-based authority, delegated voting, organizational authorization, and federated identity. AUU needs a model expressive enough to represent delegated authority, collective authority, external standing, proof-gated authority, revocation, aggregation, and rejection.

**Open evaluation questions:** Can the model explain why an actor has authority, not merely whether access is allowed? Can authority be delegated without becoming unbounded? Can a collective participate externally without laundering ineligible internal participation into eligible authority? Can authority be revoked or expired cleanly? Can outsiders challenge a claimed authority without gaining access to private identity materials?

### 5.3 Privacy-Aware Record Model

**Research question:** How can private participation produce reviewable public authority without turning records into identity, credential, association, ballot, or standing disclosures?

**Starter sources:**

* [RFC 9162: Certificate Transparency Version 2.0](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/rfc9162/) — append-only public logging model for later audit.
* [A short introduction to secrecy and verifiability for elections](https://arxiv.org/abs/1702.03168) — readable bridge between secrecy, verifiability, and voting-system analysis.
* [ElectAnon: A Blockchain-Based, Anonymous, Robust and Scalable Ranked-Choice Voting Protocol](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.00057) — example of anonymous voting with zero-knowledge components.
* [W3C Data Integrity BBS Cryptosuites v1.0](https://www.w3.org/TR/vc-di-bbs/) — useful for selective disclosure and unlinkable proof records.
* [IETF RFC 9901: Selective Disclosure for JSON Web Tokens](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/rfc9901/) — useful for claim-level disclosure control.

**What to learn from them:** Contributors should separate record integrity from record exposure. AUU needs records that can prove a process happened, show which rules governed it, support challenge and review, and issue participant receipts without exposing private participation unnecessarily.

**Open evaluation questions:** What must be public for a claim of authority to be reviewable? What can be proven without disclosure? Who, if anyone, can review protected records? Can receipts prove inclusion without enabling coercion or vote-selling? Can records remain useful over time without creating a permanent map of protected participation?

### 5.4 Governance Configuration Schema

**Research question:** How can AUU allow communities to configure governance without producing processes that are technically valid but socially incomprehensible, unauditable, or impossible to compare across federated contexts?

**Starter sources:**

* [OMG BPMN 2.0 specification](https://www.omg.org/spec/BPMN/2.0/) — formal process modeling standard.
* [BPMN overview](https://www.omg.org/bpmn/) — readable orientation to process notation.
* [Open Policy Agent documentation](https://openpolicyagent.org/docs) — policy-as-code model for structured rules.
* [Snapshot documentation](https://docs.snapshot.box/) — practical governance spaces, proposals, voting systems, and voting strategies.
* [Compound Governor Bravo documentation](https://docs.compound.finance/v2/governance/) — example of proposal thresholds, voting periods, quorum, and timelocks.
* [OpenZeppelin Governor contracts](https://docs.openzeppelin.com/contracts/4.x/api/governance) — modular governance implementation patterns.

**What to learn from them:** Contributors should distinguish between procedural expressiveness and procedural legibility. AUU needs a governance schema that can declare eligibility, roles, permissions, quorum, thresholds, timelines, delegation, challenge paths, privacy settings, record outputs, and federation effects in a structured form.

**Open evaluation questions:** How much variation can the schema support before users cannot understand configured processes? Can governance templates reduce confusion without hard-coding one legitimacy theory? Can configured processes be audited by outsiders? Can federated systems compare governance processes without forcing them into identical rules?

* [Secrets, Lies, and Account Recovery](https://elie.net/static/files/secrets-lies-and-account-recovery-lessons-from-the-use-of-personal-knowledge-questions-at-google/secrets-lies-and-account-recovery-lessons-from-the-use-of-personal-knowledge-questions-at-google-paper.pdf) — account-recovery failure study.
* [Device-Bound vs. Synced Credentials: A Comparative Evaluation of Passkey Authentication](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.07380) — useful for synced passkey and provider-dependence tradeoffs.

**What to learn from them:** Contributors should compare biometric unlock, passkeys, hardware security keys, device-bound credentials, synced credentials, session authorization, key rotation, account recovery, and secure credential storage. These should be evaluated alongside usable-security research, because maximum security can reduce adoption, retention, comprehension, and sustained safe use.

**Open evaluation questions:** Which actions require fresh confirmation? Which pseudonyms need stronger protection? How should users configure pseudonym security without confusion? How should AUU handle biometric spoofing, coercive unlock, device loss, session hijacking, warning fatigue, and account recovery? When should the system warn, recommend, require confirmation, or refuse an action?

### 5.6 Compromise and Recovery State Model

**Research question:** How can AUU restore legitimate control after compromise or loss without erasing accountability, transferring authority improperly, or rewriting civic history?

**Starter sources:**

* [NIST SP 800-63B-4](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/63/b/4/final) — authenticator lifecycle, revocation, and assurance guidance.
* [Secrets, Lies, and Account Recovery](https://elie.net/static/files/secrets-lies-and-account-recovery-lessons-from-the-use-of-personal-knowledge-questions-at-google/secrets-lies-and-account-recovery-lessons-from-the-use-of-personal-knowledge-questions-at-google-paper.pdf) — account recovery risks from real deployment data.

* [W3C Decentralized Identifiers Core](https://www.w3.org/TR/did-core/) — useful for key rotation, controllers, and identity continuity questions.

**What to learn from them:** Contributors should study key recovery, threshold control, social recovery, credential revocation, session invalidation, identity continuity, transparency logs, incident response, and disputed recovery. AUU needs recovery states that distinguish active, suspended, compromised, revoked, recovered, succeeded, and disputed identities or roles.

**Open evaluation questions:** Which failures should be recoverable? Which should remain permanently visible? What happens to prior records, reputation signals, delegation chains, collective roles, and future authority after compromise? How can AUU prevent false compromise claims, successor fraud, reputation laundering, revoked credential reuse, or unauthorized recovery?

### 5.7 Adversarial Test Model

**Research question:** How can AUU test features against hostile use before those features create authority, visibility, durable records, or external legitimacy?

**Starter sources:**

* [NIST SP 800-154: Guide to Data-Centric System Threat Modeling](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/154/ipd) — data-focused threat-modeling frame.
* [Microsoft Threat Modeling Tool overview](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/security/develop/threat-modeling-tool) — practical threat-modeling workflow for developers.
* [Microsoft STRIDE threat categories](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/security/develop/threat-modeling-tool-threats) — common structure for identifying threats.
* [OWASP Foundation](https://owasp.org/) — broad open security-resource ecosystem.
* [Zero-Knowledge Proof-of-Identity](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.09093) — example of Sybil-resistant anonymous authentication using identity proofs.
* [A Taxonomy of Decentralized Identifier Methods for Practitioners](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.03367) — useful for comparing DID methods and decentralization tradeoffs.

**What to learn from them:** Contributors should build adversarial tests for identity bridging, authority laundering, proof reuse, coercion, Sybil attacks, astroturfing, overload, challenge abuse, recovery fraud, defensive capture, and network-level observation. AUU needs threat modeling that evaluates both harmful action and harmful mitigation.

**Open evaluation questions:** What does each feature enable an attacker to do? What can be abused by governments, employers, platform operators, factions, collectives, delegates, credential issuers, or hostile participants? Can abuse controls become tools of censorship, surveillance, exclusion, or institutional capture? What evidence would show that a feature is not ready for pilot use?

### 5.8 Network Traffic Privacy and Resilient Networking

**Research question:** How can AUU prevent application-level privacy from being defeated by network-level observation, infrastructure dependence, censorship, or centralized chokepoints?

**Starter sources:**

* [Tor: The Second-Generation Onion Router](https://www.usenix.org/conference/13th-usenix-security-symposium/tor-second-generation-onion-router) — foundational Tor design paper.
* [I2P: The Invisible Internet Project](https://i2p.net/) — deployed anonymous network project.
* [The Loopix Anonymity System](https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.00536) — mixnet design using cover traffic and delays.
* [Nym whitepaper](https://nym.com/nym-whitepaper.pdf) — applied mixnet architecture based partly on Loopix.
* [Yggdrasil Network](https://yggdrasil-network.github.io/) — experimental decentralized IPv6 routing scheme for large-scale mesh networking.
* [cjdns](https://github.com/cjdelisle/cjdns) — encrypted IPv6 mesh networking using public-key cryptography and distributed routing.
* [LibreMesh](https://libremesh.org/) — OpenWrt-based framework for wireless community mesh networks.
* [Meshtastic](https://meshtastic.org/) — off-grid, low-power decentralized mesh network for peer-to-peer communication.
* [Towards Smart Community Networks](https://upcommons.upc.edu/bitstreams/24cfdf81-84d4-4cd1-bdfd-33e7ece6d4b5/download) — research overview of community-network design, routing, scalability, security, deployment, measurement, and services.
* [Community Networks and Sustainability](https://arxiv.org/abs/1707.06898) — survey of community-network practices, incentives, governance, and sustainability challenges.

**What to learn from them:** Contributors should distinguish privacy networks, anonymous communication systems, mesh overlays, wireless community networks, off-grid communication tools, and decentralized infrastructure projects. AUU needs to understand which approaches protect against traffic correlation, which support local resilience, which reduce dependency on centralized ISPs or platforms, and which merely shift trust to new operators, routing layers, or physical infrastructure.

**Open evaluation questions:** Which AUU actions require anonymity, which require resilience, and which require both? Can voting, credential proof submission, sealed-roster participation, secret-pseudonym use, delegation changes, and high-risk challenges be protected under hostile network conditions? Can community mesh networks support civic continuity during outages, censorship, or institutional deplatforming? What privacy claims are unsafe to make on mesh networks where local observers, node operators, or routing metadata may still expose participants?


### 5.9 Source Review Method

The research index should eventually track the review status of each source. A useful source entry should include:

* **Title**
* **Link**
* **Type:** standard, protocol, paper, implementation, study, case report, or documentation
* **Difficulty:** introductory, intermediate, expert
* **Relevant AUU abstraction**
* **Useful for**
* **Risks or limitations**
* **Review status:** unread, skimmed, reviewed, cited, rejected, superseded
* **Notes for specification**

A source should not be cited merely because it exists. It should explain something AUU needs to understand, provide a candidate mechanism, clarify a risk, or help reject an unsuitable approach.

### 5.10 Research Output Expectations

Research contributors should produce outputs that help AUU make decisions. Useful outputs include annotated bibliographies, comparison tables, implementation notes, threat notes, terminology summaries, failure analyses, and short decision recommendations.

A good research contribution should answer practical questions:

* Which AUU abstraction does this source inform?
* What problem does it help solve?
* What assumptions does it make?
* What risks does it introduce?
* What does it fail to address?
* Is it mature enough for specification, prototype testing, or only further research?

The purpose of Section 5 is not to make AUU appear technically complete. Its purpose is to prevent technical completion from being claimed before the relevant research has been read, compared, tested, and connected to specification work.

## 6. Specification Maturity and Decision Process

AUU should not treat every idea, feature, protocol, research candidate, or implementation approach as equally settled. The project should therefore use a lightweight maturity model adapted from technology-readiness, process-maturity, and standards-development practices. The purpose is not to copy any one external model, but to give AUU a clear way to distinguish concepts, research targets, candidate approaches, provisional specifications, prototypes, pilots, production-eligible mechanisms, and rejected or deprecated approaches.

This matters because AUU combines technical, social, institutional, and civic risks. A mechanism may be technically plausible but not yet usable. It may be usable but not yet adversarially tested. It may work in a prototype but remain unsafe for high-stakes civic use. Maturity labels should make those distinctions visible before contributors, users, or institutions mistake exploratory work for settled architecture.

### 6.1 Specification Maturity Levels

Each major abstraction, module, protocol, or implementation approach should be assigned a maturity level.

| Level                     | Meaning                                                                                                                   |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Concept                   | The idea is part of AUU’s design language but has not yet been researched or specified.                                   |
| Research target           | The problem is defined well enough for focused research and comparison.                                                   |
| Candidate approach        | One or more possible technical approaches have been identified, but no specification has been chosen.                     |
| Provisional specification | A concrete specification exists, but it has not yet been validated through implementation or adversarial testing.         |
| Prototype-tested          | The specification has been implemented in a limited reference prototype and tested against basic requirements.            |
| Adversarially tested      | The specification has been tested against abuse, compromise, coercion, correlation, overload, or capture scenarios.       |
| Pilot-tested              | The specification has been used in a limited real-world or simulated civic pilot.                                         |
| Production-eligible       | The specification has enough research, testing, review, and operational evidence to be considered for serious deployment. |
| Deprecated or rejected    | The approach has been superseded, shown unsafe, shown impractical, or rejected for strategic reasons.                     |

These levels should be visible in project documentation. They are not bureaucratic decoration. They prevent contributors from mistaking a promising research candidate for an implementation decision.

### 6.2 Research and Architecture Decision Records

Major research, technical, and architectural choices should produce decision records. These records are about the development of AUU itself, not civic decisions made by future AUU users or collectives.

The GitHub issue tracker should serve as the public working surface for research questions, objections, implementation problems, source review, proposed changes, and competing approaches. Issues may lead to decisions, but they should not replace decision records. When a significant choice is made, the decision should be summarized in a stable record rather than left buried in discussion.

A decision record should briefly state the decision, the alternatives considered, the assumptions behind the decision, the risks introduced, the expected benefits, the cost of reversal, unresolved objections, and the conditions under which the decision should be revisited.

Decision records are especially important for choices involving identity, eligibility proofs, pseudonym architecture, voting privacy, authority scoping, network traffic privacy, recovery, federation, records, security controls, and abuse resistance. These decisions affect trust in the system and should not be hidden inside code, private conversation, issue threads, or undocumented maintainer preference.

### 6.3 Assumptions and Failure Conditions

AUU should track assumptions that remain unproven. Examples include assumptions about user comprehension, credential privacy, pseudonym usability, collective governance configuration, challenge-process safety, network privacy, recovery behavior, and resistance to institutional capture.

An assumption should be paired with a failure condition where possible. The project should ask: what evidence would show that this assumption is false? A system that cannot name what would disprove its confidence is not yet ready for high-stakes civic use.

### 6.4 Reversibility and Deprecation

Early AUU specifications should prefer reversibility where possible. Credentials, pseudonym models, governance schemas, voting records, federation rules, and recovery mechanisms may need to change as research, testing, or pilots reveal weaknesses.

Deprecation should be treated as part of the strategy, not as an embarrassment. A deprecated mechanism should remain documented with the reason for replacement, the risks it introduced, and any migration path for records, users, collectives, or dependent tools.

## 7. Reference Implementation Strategy

AUU should not begin by building a full civic platform. The first implementation goal should be a set of small reference artifacts that test the abstractions identified in Section 4. These artifacts should be narrow, inspectable, replaceable, and designed to expose assumptions before larger systems are built on top of them.

Not every reference artifact is a user-facing prototype. Some may be flows, evaluators, schemas, state machines, policy models, adapters, harnesses, or integration demonstrators. These artifacts are not a fixed implementation order. Some may be merged, split, delayed, or replaced as research and decision records clarify the architecture.

Their shared purpose is to make AUU’s abstractions testable before they are embedded in a full civic platform. Success at this stage should create evidence for specification work, not claims of production readiness.

### 7.1 Eligibility-Check Reference Flow

**Artifact:** A reference flow for eligibility checks.

**Function:** Test whether a process can request eligibility proof, receive a participant response, verify the result, produce limited audit metadata, and issue a participant receipt without storing raw private information.

**Validation focus:** The flow should demonstrate at least one simple attestation-based eligibility path and one stronger privacy-preserving path. It should test expiry, revocation, duplicate-use prevention, challenge handling, mistaken acceptance, and the boundary between public process records and private qualification evidence.

**Limit:** A working eligibility-check flow should not imply that the chosen proof method is final, unlinkable, coercion-resistant, or safe for high-stakes civic use.

### 7.2 Authority-Scope Reference Evaluator

**Artifact:** A reference evaluator for authority claims.

**Function:** Test whether AUU can determine whether a participant, pseudonym, delegate, collective, credential, or prior decision has authority to perform a specific action in a specific process.

**Validation focus:** The evaluator should test direct authority, delegated authority, collective authority, proof-gated authority, expiry, revocation, aggregation, rejection, and context mismatch. It should include cases where authority is valid internally but invalid externally.

**Limit:** A working authority evaluator should not imply that federation, delegation chains, collective voting power, or representative claims are ready for durable use. It should only show that authority claims can be represented and evaluated under defined rules.

### 7.3 Privacy-Aware Record Reference Model

**Artifact:** A reference model for privacy-aware civic records.

**Function:** Test whether a process can produce reviewable records without exposing protected participation.

**Validation focus:** The model should test public metadata, private evidence, restricted review, participant receipts, challenge records, audit trails, and record integrity. It should include at least one process involving secret ballots, protected membership, private credentials, or pseudonymous participation.

**Limit:** A working record model should not imply that the privacy model is complete. In particular, it should not be treated as proof that protected rosters, secret ballots, or credential-gated processes are safe under adversarial conditions.

### 7.4 Governance Configuration Reference Schema

**Artifact:** A reference schema for structured governance configuration.

**Function:** Test whether a community can define a process through structured rules rather than informal settings or opaque platform defaults.

**Validation focus:** The schema should support eligibility rules, roles, permissions, voting method, quorum, thresholds, timelines, delegation rules, challenge paths, privacy settings, record outputs, and federation effects. It should test whether ordinary users can understand what the configured process means before participating.

**Limit:** A technically valid configuration should not imply that the process is socially understandable, legitimate, fair, or safe for consequential use.

### 7.5 Authentication and Security-Control Reference Prototype

**Artifact:** A reference prototype for wallet, pseudonym, session, device, credential, and sensitive-action security controls.

**Function:** Test whether AUU can define configurable security controls without relying on unsafe defaults, excessive friction, hidden platform enforcement, or a single device as the user’s only path to access.

**Validation focus:** The policy should test wallet access, pseudonym protection, session authorization, credential disclosure, device authorization, sensitive-action confirmation, warnings, overrides, hard restrictions, and recovery prompts. It should distinguish biometric unlock from cryptographic control and wallet recovery. A biometric may unlock a local device or authenticator, but the system should not assume that the biometric itself is a portable wallet key, a stable cross-device signature, or a sufficient recovery mechanism.

**Limit:** Stronger authentication should not be treated as automatically better, and biometric convenience should not be mistaken for durable identity control. A successful policy must show that users can understand and sustain the control model, including what happens when a device is lost, replaced, compromised, or no longer supports the same authentication method.

### 7.6 Compromise, Revocation, and Recovery State Machine

**Artifact:** A reference state machine for compromise, revocation, and recovery.

**Function:** Test whether AUU can represent lost access, compromised pseudonyms, revoked credentials, withdrawn delegation, invalid sessions, successor identities, disputed recovery claims, and record correction without erasing accountability.

**Validation focus:** The state machine should test transitions such as active, suspended, compromised, revoked, recovered, succeeded, and disputed. It should show what changes, what remains visible, what authority is invalidated, and what records or warnings persist after compromise or recovery. It should also identify where recovery depends on security-control decisions such as registered devices, cryptographic keys, recovery credentials, trusted-device approval, or other recovery paths.

**Limit:** A working recovery model should not imply that recovery is safe from abuse. It must be tested against false compromise claims, successor fraud, reputation laundering, revoked credential reuse, and unauthorized authority transfer.

### 7.7 Adversarial Test Harness

**Artifact:** A reusable test harness for attacking AUU reference artifacts under hostile conditions.

**Function:** Expose failure modes before features create authority, visibility, durable records, or external legitimacy. The harness should evolve alongside the reference artifacts rather than appear only after they are complete.

**Validation focus:** The harness should test identity bridging, authority laundering, proof reuse, coercion, Sybil behavior, challenge spam, credential abuse, session compromise, recovery fraud, collective capture, overload, and network-level observation.

**Limit:** Passing adversarial tests should not imply that a feature is safe for high-stakes civic use. It should only show that known failure modes were tested under defined assumptions.

### 7.8 Network Privacy and Resilience Reference Adapter

**Artifact:** A reference adapter for sensitive-action routing, network privacy behavior, and resilient network paths.

**Function:** Test whether AUU can reduce exposure from network-level observation and avoid total dependence on centralized infrastructure where sensitive participation, censorship resistance, or local civic continuity require stronger guarantees.

**Validation focus:** The adapter should test protected submission paths, metadata minimization, conservative logging, delayed or batched submission, privacy-aware notifications, degraded-network behavior, and user-facing warnings. It should also distinguish anonymity, traffic privacy, censorship resistance, mesh networking, and infrastructure resilience. These goals may overlap, but they are not interchangeable.

**Limit:** Network privacy and resilience artifacts should not promise anonymity. Mesh or decentralized networks may improve availability or reduce dependency on centralized infrastructure while still exposing participants through local observation, routing metadata, endpoint behavior, or operator visibility. The artifact should define which observation risks are reduced, which risks remain, and what assumptions must hold for the protection to matter.

### 7.9 Integration Demonstrator

**Artifact:** A limited demonstrator combining multiple reference artifacts in one constrained, low-stakes civic process.

**Function:** Test whether eligibility checks, authority scoping, privacy-aware records, governance configuration, security controls, recovery behavior, network privacy, resilience assumptions, and adversarial testing can operate together without contradictory assumptions.

**Validation focus:** The demonstrator should include eligibility, scoped participation, a configured decision rule, a record output, participant receipts, a challenge path, and at least one simulated failure or abuse scenario. It should be narrow enough that integration failures can be understood rather than hidden inside a broad platform build.

**Limit:** An integration demonstrator should not be treated as an MVP or pilot-ready platform. Its purpose is to reveal integration failures before pilots create false confidence.



## 8. Pilot Portfolio and Validation Roadmap

Pilots should test bundles of AUU abstractions in increasingly realistic civic situations. Section 7 defines reference artifacts; Section 8 describes how those artifacts can be combined into limited pilots. A pilot should not merely demonstrate that an interface works. It should test whether eligibility, authority, privacy, records, governance configuration, recovery, security controls, challenge paths, and adversarial assumptions remain coherent when people use them together.

Pilots should remain scoped, reversible, and honest about their maturity. Each pilot should state what it tests, what it deliberately simplifies, what risks remain, and what evidence would justify moving to a higher-stakes pilot.

### 8.1 Casual Consensus Pilot

**Use case:** A low-stakes group uses AUU to run informal polls, lightweight discussions, and non-binding sentiment checks.

**Core threads:** Pseudonymous participation, simple records, basic discussion structure, low-risk polling, participant receipts, and user comprehension.

**Validation focus:** Test whether ordinary users understand the difference between discussion, polling, and formal authority. This pilot should show whether AUU can support participation without overwhelming users with governance complexity.

**Deliberately simplified:** Eligibility, authority scoping, delegation, protected rosters, high-risk privacy, and binding votes.

**Trajectory:** If successful, this pilot becomes the foundation for deliberation-to-decision workflows.

### 8.2 Deliberation-to-Decision Pilot

**Use case:** A group connects discussion, proposals, arguments, objections, public challenges, and advisory decisions in one process.

**Core threads:** Deliberation records, proposal structure, argument visibility, challenge records, advisory polls or votes, and process auditability.

**Validation focus:** Test whether participants can follow how discussion becomes a decision record. The pilot should show whether objections, unresolved challenges, and relevant arguments remain visible without making the process unusable.

**Deliberately simplified:** Strong eligibility proofs, federation, protected participation, and binding authority.

**Trajectory:** If successful, this pilot becomes the basis for collective governance and public accountability workflows.

### 8.3 Credential-Gated Participation Pilot

**Use case:** A process restricts participation to people who satisfy a defined eligibility condition without exposing the private basis of qualification.

**Core threads:** Eligibility-check interface, privacy-preserving proof, limited audit metadata, participant receipts, expiry, revocation, and mistaken-acceptance handling.

**Validation focus:** Test whether users can prove eligibility while understanding what was proven, what was recorded, what remained private, and what risks remain.

**Deliberately simplified:** Binding authority, complex delegation, federation, and high-risk adversarial conditions.

**Trajectory:** If successful, this pilot supports proof-gated voting, protected membership, affected-party standing, and federation participation.

### 8.4 Delegation and Authority Scope Pilot

**Use case:** Participants delegate limited authority for a specific topic, process, role, or decision type, then review or revoke that delegation.

**Core threads:** Authority-scope evaluator, delegation rules, revocation, vote or action review, standing limits, and participation receipts.

**Validation focus:** Test whether delegated authority remains understandable, bounded, reviewable, and revocable. The pilot should include cases where authority is valid in one process but invalid in another.

**Deliberately simplified:** Large federations, protected rosters, high-stakes votes, and complex credential-gated authority.

**Trajectory:** If successful, this pilot supports representation, proxy voting, role-based governance, and delegated collective action.

### 8.5 Collective Governance Pilot

**Use case:** A collective defines membership rules, runs internal discussions, holds polls or votes, creates records, receives challenges, and manages roles.

**Core threads:** Governance configuration schema, membership rules, internal authority, privacy-aware records, public claims, challenge paths, role administration, and recovery scenarios.

**Validation focus:** Test whether a collective can govern itself through structured rules while producing records others can understand and challenge. The pilot should include simulated compromise, role loss, or disputed recovery.

**Deliberately simplified:** Federation, external standing, binding legal authority, and high-risk protected participation.

**Trajectory:** If successful, this pilot becomes the basis for federated collectives and external representative claims.

### 8.6 Federation and External Standing Pilot

**Use case:** Multiple collectives participate in a shared process, federation, or external decision while preserving the difference between internal membership and external standing.

**Core threads:** Authority scoping, collective standing, federation rules, proof-gated participation, representative claims, audit records, and public challenge.

**Validation focus:** Test whether collectives can act externally without laundering ineligible internal participation into eligible external voting power, representation, or standing.

**Deliberately simplified:** High-risk secrecy, binding legal effect, hostile institutional conditions, and full network-resilience requirements.

**Trajectory:** If successful, this pilot supports federated governance, inter-collective coordination, and legitimacy claims across communities.

### 8.7 Protected Participation and Network Resilience Pilot

**Use case:** A sensitive group or process uses AUU with protected membership, secret or context-specific pseudonyms, privacy-aware records, and stronger network assumptions.

**Core threads:** Protected rosters, secret-pseudonym use, network traffic privacy, privacy-aware notifications, conservative logging, degraded-network behavior, recovery limits, and user-facing risk warnings.

**Validation focus:** Test whether AUU can support sensitive participation without giving users false confidence. The pilot should distinguish anonymity, traffic privacy, censorship resistance, mesh networking, and infrastructure resilience rather than treating them as the same protection.

**Deliberately simplified:** Real dissident use, severe state adversaries, binding public authority, and guarantees of anonymity.

**Trajectory:** If successful, this pilot informs high-risk participation design, protected collectives, sealed rosters, and resilient civic infrastructure.

### 8.8 Binding Vote Simulation

**Use case:** A full-stack simulation of a consequential vote without real binding authority.

**Core threads:** Eligibility, authority scoping, ballot privacy, vote records, participation receipts, challenge paths, recovery behavior, security controls, network privacy assumptions, and adversarial testing.

**Validation focus:** Test whether AUU can run a vote that is understandable, auditable, privacy-aware, and resistant to obvious failure modes. The simulation should include at least one eligibility challenge, one authority dispute, one recovery or compromise scenario, and one network-privacy concern.

**Deliberately simplified:** Real legal authority, irreversible consequences, large-scale deployment, and claims of production readiness.

**Trajectory:** If successful, this pilot identifies what remains before AUU-derived systems could responsibly support binding institutional or civic decisions.

## 9. Adoption Pathways by Readiness Level

AUU should support incremental adoption without encouraging premature dependence. Different groups may be able to use different parts of the system at different maturity levels, but adoption should remain tied to the specification maturity process in Section 6 and the pilot evidence described in Section 8.

The goal is not to create a single launch moment where AUU becomes “ready.” The goal is to identify which uses are appropriate at each level of maturity, which uses require more evidence, and which uses should remain unavailable until the relevant mechanisms have been tested under realistic and adversarial conditions.

| Adoption context     | Appropriate early use                                                                  | Required maturity before serious use             | Major warning                                                    |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Informal groups      | Discussion, lightweight polls, advisory sentiment, simple records                      | Prototype-tested                                 | Do not confuse participation with authority.                     |
| Civil society groups | Deliberation records, public challenges, advisory decisions, non-binding governance    | Pilot-tested                                     | Records and public claims may affect real people.                |
| Collectives          | Membership rules, internal governance, delegation, role management                     | Adversarially tested                             | Internal authority must not leak into external standing.         |
| Federations          | Shared processes, representative claims, external standing, inter-collective decisions | Pilot-tested or production-eligible              | Prevent authority laundering across collectives.                 |
| High-risk groups     | Limited privacy and resilience testing only                                            | Production-eligible for safety-critical features | Do not promise anonymity or protection that has not been proven. |
| Institutions         | Binding or semi-binding decisions, official representation, durable public authority   | Production-eligible                              | Do not create premature legitimacy claims.                       |

### 9.1 Mock Adoption Studies

The following examples are not predictions, endorsements, or complete product proposals. They are structured adoption studies. Their purpose is to show how AUU-derived tools might be used at different maturity levels, where each use should stop, and which risks should prevent premature escalation.

#### 9.1.1 Informal Community Polling

A small online community uses AUU-derived tools to run informal polls, organize discussion, and preserve simple participation records. The group wants better memory and clearer sentiment, not formal authority.

This is an appropriate early use if the polling, discussion, pseudonym, and record features are at least prototype-tested. The process should remain explicitly non-binding. Users should understand that participation records show what happened inside the group, not what the group is authorized to impose on others.

The adoption boundary is authority. If poll results begin to be treated as mandates, representative claims, disciplinary justification, or public proof of collective will, the use has exceeded its maturity level.

#### 9.1.2 Civil Society Accountability Campaign

A civil society group uses AUU-derived tools to gather proposals, record objections, track public challenges, and show how an advisory position was reached. The group wants a more accountable process than a private chat thread or social media poll.

This use may be appropriate after deliberation-to-decision pilots show that participants can understand proposal structure, challenge records, unresolved objections, and advisory outcomes. The process may create useful public memory, but it should not imply formal representation unless authority has been separately established.

The adoption boundary is public legitimacy. A campaign may use AUU-derived records to show its own process, but it should not treat those records as proof that affected people, members, experts, or the public have granted authority.

#### 9.1.3 Collective Internal Governance

A cooperative, association, online community, or local group uses AUU-derived tools to define membership rules, assign roles, hold internal votes, manage delegation, and preserve governance records.

This use requires stronger maturity than informal polling because internal governance can affect real membership, status, access, responsibility, and trust. It should depend on tested governance configuration, authority scoping, privacy-aware records, role management, recovery handling, and challenge paths.

The adoption boundary is external standing. A collective may govern itself internally, but internal participation should not automatically become external voting power, public representation, federation standing, or proof of legitimacy outside the collective.

#### 9.1.4 Federated Coalition Process

Several collectives use AUU-derived tools to coordinate a shared statement, joint campaign, resource allocation, or inter-collective decision. Each collective has its own internal rules, but the federation needs a shared process for external action.

This use should come after collective governance, credential-gated participation, authority disputes, public challenge, and federation pilots. The system must distinguish internal membership from external standing and must make representative claims reviewable without exposing protected participants unnecessarily.

The adoption boundary is authority laundering. A federation should not allow a collective to convert ineligible internal participation into eligible external voting power, representation, or standing.

#### 9.1.5 Protected Participation Scenario

A sensitive group tests protected membership, secret or context-specific pseudonyms, privacy-aware records, network traffic privacy warnings, resilient-network assumptions, and conservative logging under controlled conditions.

This should be treated as safety-critical testing, not ordinary adoption. The purpose is to discover failure modes, not to prove that the system protects vulnerable users. The pilot should distinguish anonymity, traffic privacy, censorship resistance, mesh networking, and infrastructure resilience rather than treating them as one protection.

The adoption boundary is user safety. AUU-derived tools should not be offered as protection against employers, hostile institutions, violent actors, or states unless the relevant safety-critical mechanisms are production-eligible and their limits are clearly communicated.

#### 9.1.6 Institutional Binding Vote Attempt

An institution wants to use AUU-derived tools for a binding or semi-binding decision, such as leadership selection, policy ratification, public representation, funding allocation, or official mandate.

This should be the latest form of adoption. It requires production-eligible maturity for eligibility, authority scoping, ballot privacy, voting records, auditability, challenge processes, recovery, security controls, and adversarial resistance. It also requires decision records explaining why the chosen mechanisms are appropriate and what risks remain.

The adoption boundary is binding authority. If the relevant mechanisms are not mature enough, the institution may run a simulation, advisory process, or internal learning exercise, but it should not treat the result as binding merely because AUU-derived tools were used.

### 9.2 Adoption Principle

AUU should grow by matching use to maturity. Low-risk tools may be useful before the full system exists, but high-stakes authority should wait for evidence. The project should prefer slow, honest adoption over premature legitimacy, especially where failure could expose people, distort representation, launder authority, or damage trust in civic infrastructure.

## 10. Threat Validation and Red-Team Review

Every AUU feature that creates identity, authority, privacy, records, reach, or legitimacy also creates an attack surface. The project should therefore treat threat validation as a continuous development requirement rather than a late security audit.

AUU should not ask only whether a feature works for cooperative users. It should ask what the feature allows hostile users, careless users, institutions, collectives, delegates, credential issuers, operators, maintainers, and outside observers to do. A feature should not advance in maturity merely because it functions. It should advance only when its failure modes have been named, tested, mitigated where possible, and connected to adoption limits.

### 10.1 Threat Validation Principle

Threat validation should follow the abstractions, artifacts, pilots, and adoption pathways described earlier in this document. Section 4 identifies the integration problems. Section 7 creates reference artifacts. Section 8 tests pilot bundles. Section 9 limits adoption by maturity. Section 10 defines the discipline used to challenge confidence at each stage.

Threat validation should be applied especially to features involving identity, eligibility, pseudonyms, delegation, voting, protected rosters, public claims, federation, recovery, network privacy, audit records, analytics, and security controls. These features affect trust in the system and should not be treated as mature until adversarial review has shaped their design.

### 10.2 Threat Register

AUU should maintain a threat register connecting threat areas to affected features, attacker models, test methods, mitigations, residual risks, and maturity consequences. The register should remain visible enough for contributors to understand why certain features are delayed, restricted, redesigned, or rejected.

| Threat area                    | Example risks                                                                          | Must be tested before                |
| ------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Identity and personhood        | Sybil attacks, biometric spoofing, witness collusion, false attestations               | Credential-gated participation       |
| Pseudonym privacy              | Linkability, stylometry, metadata correlation, accidental identity bridging            | Protected participation              |
| Eligibility proofs             | Proof reuse, issuer correlation, revocation failure, mistaken acceptance               | Proof-gated voting or standing       |
| Authority scoping              | Authority laundering, delegation abuse, federation overreach, role misuse              | Collective and federation pilots     |
| Voting and coercion            | Vote buying, forced proof, receipt misuse, ballot exposure, coercive delegation        | Binding vote simulation              |
| Records and auditability       | Overexposure, reconstruction attacks, unverifiable claims, missing objections          | Public claims and challenge records  |
| Collective governance          | Hostile takeover, exclusion, moderation capture, role capture, process manipulation    | Collective governance adoption       |
| Federation                     | Ineligible aggregation, representative inflation, ambiguous standing, contested claims | Inter-collective decisions           |
| Security and recovery          | Device loss, false compromise, malicious recovery, revoked credential reuse            | Authority-bearing use                |
| Network privacy and resilience | Timing correlation, endpoint logs, denial of service, chokepoints, false anonymity     | Protected participation              |
| Analytics and AI               | Re-identification, bias, manipulation, adversarial prompts, false consensus maps       | Public summaries or decision support |
| Supply chain                   | Malicious clients, backdoored dependencies, compromised updates, rollback attacks      | Serious deployment                   |

The threat register should not be limited to technical attacks. AUU also needs to test social, institutional, procedural, and interpretive failures. A technically correct system can still fail if users misunderstand authority, if collectives launder legitimacy, if records expose vulnerable participants, or if moderation and safety tools become instruments of capture.

### 10.3 Red-Team Review Types

AUU should use multiple forms of red-team review because no single review method covers the full risk surface.

**Security review** should examine authentication, wallet control, session management, device trust, credential storage, recovery, dependency risk, malicious updates, and compromise handling.

**Privacy review** should examine identity bridging, pseudonym linkability, credential exposure, roster protection, ballot secrecy, traffic correlation, logging, analytics, and re-identification.

**Governance review** should examine authority scoping, delegation, role assignment, quorum, thresholds, federation rules, public claims, institutional capture, and authority laundering.

**Abuse review** should examine spam, overload, harassment, challenge abuse, bad-faith participation, coordinated manipulation, Sybil behavior, and moderation capture.

**Usability review** should examine whether users understand what they are doing, what authority they are granting, what they are revealing, what is recorded, what can be challenged, and what cannot be undone.

**Institutional review** should examine how AUU-derived tools could be misused by employers, governments, platforms, political factions, organizations, or powerful collectives to create false legitimacy, suppress dissent, expose opponents, or manufacture consent.

### 10.4 Residual Risk and Maturity Gates

Threats do not need to be eliminated before a feature can progress, but they must be understood. A feature may advance with residual risk only if that risk is named, bounded, documented, and reflected in the feature’s maturity label.

A feature should not be marked pilot-tested merely because it has been used by cooperative participants. It should be tested against realistic failure scenarios. A feature should not be marked production-eligible merely because known attacks have mitigations. It should have evidence that the mitigations work, that users understand the remaining risks, and that adoption boundaries prevent premature reliance.

Where threat validation exposes severe unresolved risk, the correct result may be delay, redesign, restricted use, or rejection. AUU should prefer visible incompleteness over false maturity.

### 10.5 Review Outputs

Serious reviews should produce stable records rather than informal impressions. A review record should identify the affected feature, threat area, attacker model, test method, finding, mitigation, residual risk, maturity impact, unresolved objections, and revisit condition.

Review outputs should connect back to decision records. If a risky approach is accepted, the reason should be documented. If an approach is rejected, the rejection should remain visible enough to prevent later contributors from unknowingly reopening the same failure path. If a mitigation depends on assumptions that may change, those assumptions should be stated explicitly.

Threat validation should create institutional memory for the project. The goal is not to prove that AUU is safe in the abstract. The goal is to make risk visible enough that development, pilots, adoption, and future governance do not proceed on hidden confidence.

## 11. Contribution Priorities

AUU requires many kinds of contribution, but not every contribution is equally useful at every stage. Early work should prioritize clarity, research grounding, specification maturity, and reference artifacts before full application development.

Contributors should avoid treating AUU as if it already has a settled architecture. The project needs disciplined comparison, careful criticism, narrow prototypes, adversarial review, and documentation that distinguishes concepts from decisions.

| Priority            | Useful contribution                                                         | Expected output                            |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Research review     | Review standards, papers, protocols, and comparable systems                 | Annotated research notes or source reviews |
| Specification work  | Turn feature concepts into precise interfaces, schemas, and state models    | Candidate or provisional specifications    |
| Reference artifacts | Build narrow flows, evaluators, schemas, prototypes, adapters, or harnesses | Inspectable reference implementations      |
| Threat validation   | Test assumptions, abuse cases, privacy failures, and governance risks       | Threat records and red-team findings       |
| Pilot design        | Define limited pilots with clear scope, maturity, and adoption boundaries   | Pilot plans and validation criteria        |
| Documentation       | Improve clarity, terminology, examples, and contributor orientation         | Reviewed documentation changes             |
| Decision records    | Preserve major research, technical, and architectural decisions             | Stable decision records                    |

### 11.1 Research and Source Review

Research contributions should help AUU understand available approaches without prematurely selecting one. Contributors may review verifiable credentials, selective disclosure, zero-knowledge proofs, voting systems, governance tooling, identity systems, passkeys, hardware keys, mesh networking, mixnets, policy languages, recovery models, moderation systems, and comparable civic platforms.

A useful source review should explain what the source contributes, what problem it addresses, what assumptions it makes, where it may fail, and which AUU abstraction it informs. Source review should not be treated as endorsement.

### 11.2 Specification Contributions

Specification work should translate AUU’s civic concepts into implementation-facing definitions. This includes eligibility interfaces, authority-scope models, privacy-aware record models, governance configuration schemas, recovery state machines, security-control behavior, network privacy assumptions, and adversarial test cases.

A useful specification contribution should define the object, its fields or states, its allowed transitions, its failure modes, its privacy implications, its audit outputs, and its relationship to other abstractions. Specification work should remain tied to the maturity model in Section 6.

### 11.3 Reference Artifact Contributions

Reference artifacts should be narrow enough to inspect and replace. A useful artifact does not need to become the final implementation. It should test whether a concept can be represented, implemented, explained, reviewed, attacked, and integrated.

Contributors should prefer small artifacts that expose assumptions over broad prototypes that hide unresolved problems. A simple authority evaluator, recovery state machine, eligibility flow, governance schema, or adversarial test harness may be more valuable than an impressive interface that skips the hard questions.

### 11.4 Review and Criticism

Criticism is a core contribution to AUU. The project should welcome objections that identify ambiguity, unsafe assumptions, overreach, ethical risk, privacy failure, attack surfaces, institutional misuse, user confusion, or premature claims of readiness.

A useful objection should identify the affected claim or mechanism, explain the risk, distinguish speculation from evidence where possible, and suggest what would need to be clarified, tested, changed, delayed, or rejected. Repeated or previously answered objections should be connected to existing decision records or threat records rather than ignored.

### 11.5 Implementation Restraint

AUU should not reward implementation speed at the expense of architectural clarity. Many parts of the system are tempting to build before they are safe to specify. Identity, delegation, voting, federation, protected participation, public legitimacy, and recovery are especially vulnerable to premature implementation.

Contributors should treat early implementation as research support, not product launch. The best early code is code that helps the project learn what must be specified, tested, rejected, or redesigned.

## 12. Closing Principle

AUU should mature by evidence rather than confidence. Its goal is not to build civic technology quickly, nor to gather users around an unfinished promise of legitimacy. Its goal is to develop civic infrastructure carefully enough that identity, authority, privacy, deliberation, voting, delegation, records, recovery, and federation can be trusted under real social pressure.

The project should therefore remain willing to slow down where the consequences are high. Low-risk tools may become useful early. High-stakes authority should wait. Protected participation should not be promised before protection is real. Institutional adoption should not proceed by enthusiasm alone. Federation should not be allowed to launder authority. Records should not expose people in the name of accountability. Privacy should not become an excuse for unreviewable power.

A Universal Union can only become trustworthy if its development process is also trustworthy. Research, specification, implementation, pilots, adoption, and review should all make the same commitment: do not hide uncertainty, do not manufacture legitimacy, and do not let the system claim more maturity than it has earned.
